How can we design a simple, adaptable website that conveys a unified brand identity, enhances user experience, and accommodates the unique characteristics of organization centers?

By creating a modular template that ensures a consistent digital identity, provides optimized navigation through a clear information architecture, and adapts to the specific needs of each center.

01. Discover, understand, and empathize

Understanding the challenge and research objectives

  • Identify requirements and expectations through interviews with key stakeholders (Product Owner and web governance leads).
  • Analyze web references to identify user profiles, services, and best practices.
  • Research the needs and frustrations of the main users of organization websites.

Peer benchmarking

Primary Research

User interviews

Six individuals with different user profiles were interviewed: 2 internal staff members, 1 external staff member, 1 company representative, 1 media professional, and 1 public sector representative. The interviews were conducted in person and lasted approximately one hour. The questions focused on the following topics:

Key research findings

  • Lack of visual and functional consistency, weakening organization's digital identity.
  • The design must align with existing websites and comply with institutional identity guidelines and the General State Administration’s regulations.
  • Confusing navigation, lack of advanced search functionality, and difficulties accessing key contacts.
  • Outdated and poorly structured content, affecting clarity and relevance.

02. Define the challenge

Target Audience

Following the research phase, the MVP was defined to focus on the most relevant user persona: external scientific staff. The remaining user profiles (internal staff, companies, media, and the general public) will be addressed in future iterations.

User Journey

Problem statement

The key users of organization center websites, especially external scientific staff, face difficulties accessing essential information, such as project details, contact information, and how to establish communication. The project's objective was to create an easily adaptable web template that strengthens organization's identity and improves access to information and contact options, thereby enhancing the experience of the primary user defined for this MVP.

03. Ideating the solution

How Might We

The first step in designing an MVP that addressed the objectives identified during the research phase began with an exploration of how we could solve the detected problems and challenges. To achieve this, the following "How Might We" questions were formulated:

Prioritization

A combination of a usability and feasibility matrix, along with the MoSCoW method, was used to prioritize solutions, balancing the user persona's needs with technical constraints and project requirements. The MVP will include: simplified navigation, a clear structure for accessing information, enhanced contact tools, and an adaptable template that reinforces the organization’s digital identity.

View MoSCoW

Information Architecture

The homepage was defined as the core navigation hub, directly connecting to key sections such as departments, projects, publications, services, and contact, ensuring quick access in just a few clicks. The hierarchy of the research section was structured in a user-friendly way, based on common patterns found in organization websites. Additionally, the sitemap was designed to enhance connections between pages, facilitating access to related information and contact with the centers.

View information architecture

User Flow

The main user flow was designed to facilitate access to structured information about projects (Department > Groups > Projects). If the required content was not found, users needed to easily contact the research staff or the center, integrating this aspect into the interaction flow design.

View user flow

MVP approach to solve the problem statement

During this phase, the following key ideas were defined for developing an MVP that meets the needs of external scientific staff and the project objectives:

  • A modular and flexible template that reinforces the organization’s visual identity and leverages the capabilities of WordPress and its associated theme.
  • Simplified navigation with a clear and familiar menu to facilitate content access.
  • Accessibility and efficient search through quick access links and advanced filters to locate key information.
  • Direct contact via a visible button that provides easy access to forms and key staff contact details.

04. Designing with purpose

Wireframes

Low-fidelity wireframes were created for eight specific screens, designed to facilitate access to key resources such as projects, publications, and contact information. These wireframes helped define each user step, establish clear content hierarchies, and structure a modular and intuitive navigation system that enhances the experience of accessing scientific information.

View wireframes

Visual Guide and Design System

A design system was created based on an atomic approach to define the visual and functional aspects of the interface. This included strategic decisions on typography, color palette, and reusable components such as buttons and navigation elements. The system ensures a consistent digital identity, adaptable to different organization centers, and aligned with stakeholder requirements, serving as the foundation for developing the prototype.

View visual guide and design system

05. Prototyping the experience

High-Fidelity Prototype

A prototype was developed to represent the user flow across key screens. The main menu was designed as the central navigation element, while the project search included tabs with filters by department. Additionally, a contact form was created to direct inquiries to specific departments, facilitating communication with center staff.

View high-fidelity prototype

06. Testing & Validation

Usability Testing and Feedback Analysis

Usability tests were conducted with four external researchers and three stakeholders, evaluating key tasks such as project search, publication review, and contacting responsible personnel. The results confirmed good accessibility and clarity but identified areas for improvement, such as adding a fixed menu, a floating button to return to the top of the page, and a clearer, more structured way to present contact information. These findings validated the strengths of the prototype and helped prioritize adjustments to optimize the user experience.

View feedback collection

Key learnings & next steps

  • The challenges included balancing user needs with the requirements of the Product Owner and governance, addressing issues such as confusing navigation, ineffective search functionality, and inconsistent design.
  • Designing with a focus on clarity and accessibility proved essential, and unifying the organization’s visual identity is feasible without compromising flexibility for center websites.
  • The next steps involve optimizing navigation with a fixed menu and return buttons, implementing advanced filters and combined searches, adding FAQs and specific forms, and validating improvements through continuous iterations.
